Making neurodiverse-friendly video games (Research)

“Designing Neurodiverse-Friendly Games” is a project that will examine the design, enjoyment, and use of video games amongst neurodivergent people. The project aims to uncover what aspects of games are appealing and unappealing to neurodivergent folks - many of whom are notorious gamers. Once this is uncovered the project will research ways to incorporate this into game design. The project can be thought about using two key research questions;

  • What is it about video games that is appealing to neurodivergent people and how can we include more of this?
  • What is it about video games that is unappealing to neurodivergent people and how can we limit this?
The target consumer of gaming industries includes a large population of neurodivergents. In addition to this a recent study found that 18% of people employed in the gaming industry are neurodivergent (Taylor, 2022). It is therefore interesting that very little research is done on ensuring enjoyment for neurodivergents in the game design process (4% of 151 papers, according to a recent review by Spiel & Gerling, 2020!). It is only beneficial to the industry to acknowledge this and incorporate it into their game design and development methodologies. Video games and neurodivergence is a well-researched field. However, the bulk of this research is from a healthcare or educational perspective. Most (over half in one study of relevant papers) medicinal research focuses on using video games to pinpoint the perceived deficits of neurodivergent (most research is on Autism) people and correct it (Spiel & Gerling, 2020). The other main field is education. Video games are used as an educational tool to ‘teach’ neurodivergent children how to be more social or act appropriately in a classroom. Most of this research uses an outdated and negative approach - driven by a medical educational purpose. One review found that over half of them involved a majority of male autistic participants. The current research is not adequate. The primary use of video games is at-home-enjoyment, not medical or educational. Yet little research is done on how to ensure video games are appealing and accessible to neurodiverse people. Even gaming companies themselves do not conduct research into the actual enjoyment and play preferences of a huge proportion of their consumers - the neurodivergent perspective.
